This site is small on the surface and deliberately over-engineered underneath. It's a personal site, but I treat it like a product — partly because I enjoy it, partly because it's where I try ideas before they reach [Frontal](https://frontal.dev). Here's how it's built.

## A monorepo for one person

It runs as a Bun monorepo: a Next.js 16 app in `apps/web`, plus a few packages I own end to end. That's overkill for a personal site, and that's the point. Splitting concerns into packages forces clean boundaries and makes each piece easy to reason about on its own.

The two I care about most:

- **`packages/seo`** — my own SEO layer: metadata, structured data, sitemaps, and the `llms.txt` / `llms-full.txt` routes so language models can read the site in a format built for them.
- **`packages/security`** — headers, a content security policy, and the hardening I'd want on anything public-facing.

The stack is TypeScript in strict mode, Tailwind with shadcn/ui, Sentry for errors, and Vercel for hosting.

## Content as files

Posts and projects — including this one — are MDX files with typed frontmatter, validated with Zod at build time. No CMS, no database. Writing is just editing a file and committing it, which is exactly the friction level I want.

## An AI that answers as me

There's a chat on the site trained on a profile of who I am and what I'm working on. Ask it something and it answers in my voice. It's a small feature, but it's the most honest expression of what this site is for: a place that represents me even when I'm not there.

## Live, not static

The site pulls real data — what I'm listening to on Spotify, recent training from Strava, activity from GitHub. I'd rather show what's actually true right now than a frozen "about me." It makes the site feel alive, and it keeps me honest.

## The point

I build in public, and this site is part of that. It's open-source, it's a sandbox, and it's never really finished. That's the fun of it.
