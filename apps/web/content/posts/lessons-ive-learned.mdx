I don't write blog posts to make myself look smart. I write to tell you what I learned the hard way - not the sanitized version of a TED talk.

Here are the lessons I've learned building software and people.

## Technical Lessons

**Clarity isn't polish, it's work**
I used to think writing clean code was "nice to have." It's actually the only sustainable way to build software. When code becomes unclear, nobody wants to change it - which kills the system.

Code that took hours to write should be obvious to anyone reading it in a month.

**Bad code is borrowed time, not saved time**
The shortcut that saves you an hour today will cost you three hours later, like compound interest. A recent example: dismissing performance issues until after you have thousands of concurrent users. The lateness is proportional to the complexity accumulated.

**Don't confuse activity with progress**
Building a website, writing a README, or fixing a bug without thinking about the architectural implications doesn't make the system better. Understanding how everything fits together matters more than fixing everything.

## Startup Lessons

**Boring is the new awesome**
Based on my first startup being built in public, the most sustainable products aren't the flashiest ones. Simplicity sells. Work smarter. We've been explicit about what's hard to build and focused on making it right.

**Balance and trade-offs**
Balancing theory and execution is hard - but valuable, especially after being at NYU and Lisbon where the balance looks very different. What makes software engineering meaningful isn't working hard, it's being part of something larger than yourself.

**Building in public is exhausting**
Everyone wants to "build in public," but you have to pay for being exposed. You have to invest in privacy in a world that encourages self-exposure.

## Developer Experience (DX) Lessons

**Great libraries are invisible**
They don't shout about their features. They just work. People use them because they're useful, not because they're "cool." Invisible libraries become the backbone of every system.

**Developer time is expensive**
What people need isn't code, it's attention. Don't make someone have to learn your framework to build something simple. Don't ask questions that don't need answers. Don't waste time working on usability problems where users will quickly get over it.

**Good APIs are good design**
API design is part of system design. What you expose says as much about what you're building as the implementation.

## Forgetting Lessons is a Bug

Forgetting what is technically and personally difficult adds dysfunction. Lessons learned are like depreciation - they need to be accounted for in decision-making.

I try to remember:
- What happens when someone rejects a bad idea without backing
- How you feel when burned
- Who gets left behind
- What happens when someone doesn't have the training they need to do their job

## My Evolution of Understanding

I used to think we decided what to build based on filtering criteria. Instead, I've learned that what you value determines what you notice.

Questions I now ask:
- Do I read what I write?
- Can I honestly say I tried? Have I tried hard?
- What lessons am I making too complex to be explicit?
- How can I make things easier for people who weren't born with the same advantages?

## Looking Back vs. Looking Forward

I haven't solved these problems. I'm still figuring it out. I'm still playing catch-up: learning complex systems while building with deep uncertainty.

This is just what's working at the moment. I'm constantly learning.
