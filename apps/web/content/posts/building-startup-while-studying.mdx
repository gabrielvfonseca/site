I'm a Computer Engineering student at NOVA School of Science and Technology in Lisbon. I'm also the founder and CEO of Frontal, an enterprise AI company. I'm writing this from a cafe near the university because that's where I did homework last semester.

This is what "building while learning" actually looks like.

## The Reality: It's Hard

You can't treat this like a side project. Building a company while studying requires protecting deep work time, making trade-offs, and being brutally honest about what you can ship in 10-hour days instead of 60-hour weeks.

Last semester I:
- Attended every Algorithms lecture
- Built Agentic workflows in Vibe on weekends
- Talked to 12 potential customers for Frontal
- Wrote this post between classes

The trick isn't working more hours. It's working differently.

## How I Structure My Time

I protect mornings for deep work. 7-11 AM is when I code. This is when the brain is fresh and interruptions are minimal. Afternoons are for:
- University classes
- Meetings with co-founders
- Customer calls
- Reading research papers

Evenings are for:
- Writing
- Testing ideas
- Light code review

This isn't sustainable forever. When I graduate, the balance will shift. But for now, this structure is what's working.

## The Curriculum Advantage

Being a student isn't a handicap—it's a force multiplier. My coursework directly applies:

- **Distributed Systems** → Frontal's architecture
- **Compilers** → Vibe's runtime primitives
- **Algorithms** → Operator's planning engine
- **Research Methods** → Evals and testing

I'm getting paid to validate academic theories in production. That's not common.

## Shipping Before You're Ready

My professors teach theoretical correctness. Startup founder instincts teach you to ship before you're ready and learn from real users.

The Optimizer taught me that shipping in the open, with imperfect solutions, gets you more learning than perfect solutions in a vacuum.

## The Trade-offs

What I've given up:
- Late nights with friends
- Some social events
- Perfect code quality (sometimes)

What I've gained:
- Real user feedback
- A product that actually solves real problems
- Skills I couldn't get from textbooks

## Advice for Student Founders

1. **Start with university problems** — I built Frontal because I saw how hard it is for teams to actually use AI products.

2. **Class time is sacred** — Protect it like investor meetings. You need that education.

3. **Ship something monthly** — Small, consistent progress beats heroic efforts.

4. **Find student co-founders** — We're all learning the same things.

5. **Use university resources** — Labs, professors, student funds. They exist to help you.

## The Long Game

This isn't about hustle. It's about building something that lasts. I want Frontal to be a company that outlives my student days, and I want my education to give me the foundation to make it right.

The intersection of academic rigor and startup pragmatism is where interesting things happen. I'm learning to live there.

## Where Frontal Is Today

- 3 engineers (including me)
- 15 paying teams testing early features
- Product-market fit questions we're actively answering
- A roadmap that balances my graduation timeline with customer needs

I'll write another update in 6 months when I have more data. For now, this is what's working.
