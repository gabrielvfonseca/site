If I could sit down with the version of me who just wrote his first `hello world`, here's what I'd say.

Nothing dramatic. Just the things I wish someone had told me before I learned them the expensive way.

## On Code

**Write less, think more.**
You'll think writing 500 lines makes you productive. It doesn't. The best programmers I know write 50 lines that do what 500 used to. Constraints force clarity. Embrace them.

**Boring code is good code.**
That clever one-liner you're proud of? Someone will spend three hours understanding it in six months. Usually you. Write the version a tired person can read at 5pm on a Friday.

**Tests aren't optional.**
You think types catch everything. They don't. A test that fails when your logic is wrong is worth more than a type annotation that makes you feel safe.

**Learn the boring fundamentals.**
Distributed systems, databases, networking, operating systems. Everyone wants to skip to AI. But when your agent falls over in production, the boring knowledge is what saves you.

## On Learning

**Depth beats breadth.**
You'll try to learn Rust, TypeScript, Python, and AI all at once. You'll end up shallow in all of them. Pick one. Go deep. The second one gets easier because you understand the patterns.

**Read the source.**
Tutorials show you how. Source code shows you why. When something breaks, the answer is almost always in the code, not the docs.

**Build things, not courses.**
A course gives you a certificate. A project gives you judgment. Ship something broken to real users. That's where learning actually happens.

**Read daily, a little every day.**
Three books a year beats zero books a year. Consistency compounds. The day you stop reading is the day you start repeating yourself.

## On Career

**Find the boring problem nobody's solving.**
Everyone chases the flashy demo. The money and the meaning are in the unglamorous infrastructure everyone depends on but nobody celebrates. Build that.

**Talk to users before you write code.**
You'll build things nobody wants. Every time. Build something small, show it to a real person, watch them struggle with it. That's your curriculum.

**Protect your deep work.**
Mornings are sacred. Meetings and email will eat them if you let them. Guard them like they're your most valuable asset, because they are.

**Reputation is built on keeping commitments.**
Don't over-promise to investors, employers, or users. Under-promise and over-deliver. Trust is the only currency that matters long-term.

## On Building

**Ship smaller things that work.**
Not one big thing that's "almost done." A small thing that works is worth ten half-built monuments to your ambition.

**Build in public, but protect your privacy.**
Share what you learn. It helps others and forces you to think clearly. But you don't owe the internet your whole life. Boundaries are healthy.

**Simplicity is a feature.**
Every feature you add is a feature you have to maintain, document, and explain. Say no more than you say yes.

**You're not your code.**
When someone criticizes your architecture, they're not criticizing you. Detach. The code is a draft. You can rewrite it.

## On Life

**Balance isn't a luxury, it's a requirement.**
You'll think grinding 80-hour weeks makes you serious. It makes you burnt out. Rest isn't the reward for work. It's part of the work.

**Long walks solve more than Stack Overflow.**
Most of your breakthroughs will come on a walk, not at the keyboard. The brain processes in the background. Give it space.

**Drink less coffee.**
14 coffees a week is not a personality trait. It's a heartbeat problem waiting to happen.

**The people matter more than the product.**
You'll remember the teammates, not the deploy. Choose who you build with as carefully as what you build.

## On AI

**It's a tool, not a oracle.**
Stop treating models like they're magic. They're software with a probabilistic core. Build systems that verify, audit, and constrain them. That's the real work.

**Agents are systems, not chatbots.**
When you build agents, you're building distributed systems. Plan accordingly. Memory, context, validation - those are the primitives, not prompts.

**Build for the next 100 years.**
Trends die. Fundamentals don't. The code you write should still make sense in a decade. Boring, clear, correct - that's what lasts.

## The Last Thing

Don't rush. You have time. The version of you at 30 will be smarter than the version at 20, and that's fine. You're not behind. You're exactly where you need to be to learn what's next.

Now go write the boring version.
