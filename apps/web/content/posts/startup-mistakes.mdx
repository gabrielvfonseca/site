I've made most of the classic startup mistakes. Some I caught early. Some cost months.

Here's the autopsy, so you don't repeat them.

## Product Mistakes

### 1. Building for "Enterprise" Before Having a Single User

**What I did:** Designed multi-tenant architecture, RBAC, audit logs, SSO integration before talking to 5 potential customers.

**Why:** "Enterprise requirements" felt like real work. Talking to humans felt scary.

**Cost:** 3 months of features nobody asked for.

**Fix:** First 10 customers = manual onboarding, shared instance, no SSO. Build auth when someone *pays* and *asks* for it.

### 2. Confusing "Cool Tech" with "Valuable Problem"

**What I did:** Built Operator (agentic framework) with complex planning, memory, tool use. Spent months on the "how."

**Why:** The tech was intellectually satisfying. The demo was impressive.

**Reality:** Customers wanted "reliable extraction from PDFs" not "autonomous agents that plan multi-step workflows."

**Fix:** Start with the ugliest, most specific problem someone will pay to solve. Wrap the cool tech *inside* that solution.

### 3. Ignoring Distribution Until Launch

**What I did:** "Build it, they will come." Zero content, zero outreach, zero pipeline until product was "ready."

**Why:** Distribution felt like "marketing fluff." I'm an engineer.

**Cost:** Launch day = 0 signups. 6 weeks to first paying customer.

**Fix:** Distribution starts day 1. Write about the problem. Talk to 100 people. Build the waitlist *while* building the product.

## People Mistakes

### 4. Hiring for Skills, Not Values Alignment

**What I did:** Hired a brilliant engineer who optimized for technical elegance over shipping. Beautiful code, missed every deadline.

**Why:** "They're so smart!" I ignored that they'd never shipped a product.

**Cost:** 4 months of velocity drag, team morale hit, painful separation.

**Fix:** Hire for: shipping track record, communication, ownership. Test with a paid 2-week project before full offer.

### 5. Not Firing Fast Enough

**What I did:** Kept someone 3 months past the point I knew it wouldn't work. "They'll improve." "It's hard to hire."

**Why:** Conflict avoidance. Sunk cost. Hope.

**Cost:** Team saw I tolerated underperformance. The person knew and was miserable anyway.

**Fix:** 30-60-90 day plan with explicit milestones. If not met at 30, have the conversation. At 60, decide. At 90, act.

### 6. Treating Advisors as Decoration

**What I did:** Got 5 impressive advisors. Talked to them quarterly. Asked generic questions.

**Why:** "Advisors = credibility for investors."

**Reality:** Advisors who don't engage are useless. Credibility comes from *their* results, not their names on your deck.

**Fix:** 1-2 advisors with specific domain expertise. Monthly 30-min calls with prepared agenda. Equity vests monthly based on engagement.

## Strategy Mistakes

### 7. Raising Money Before Product-Market Fit

**What I did:** Raised pre-seed on vision + team. Spent 60% of time fundraising for 6 months.

**Why:** "Everyone raises early." Felt like validation.

**Cost:** 6 months not talking to users. Money created pressure to "execute" on wrong things.

**Fix:** Raise when you have: 10+ paying customers, clear retention, repeatable sales motion. Or don't raise at all.

### 8. Chasing Competitors' Features

**What I did:** Competitor launched "vector search." We pivoted sprint to add vector search. Nobody used it.

**Why:** Fear of missing out. Investor pressure.

**Fix:** Competitors are noise. Customers are signal. If 3+ customers ask for X, build X. Otherwise, ignore.

### 9. Optimizing for Valuation, Not Value

**What I did:** Structured raise to maximize cap. Took money from fund that wanted board seat + aggressive growth.

**Why:** Ego. "Higher valuation = success."

**Reality:** Wrong partner > lower valuation. Board seat with misaligned incentives = lost control.

**Fix:** Optimize for: partner quality, alignment, flexibility. Valuation is a number. Control is survival.

## Execution Mistakes

### 10. No Weekly Review Cadence

**What I did:** Sprint planning Monday. Retro... never. Just "keep shipping."

**Why:** "Process is bureaucracy."

**Reality:** Without reflection, you optimize for activity, not outcomes.

**Fix:** Every Friday: What moved revenue? What didn't? What do we stop? What do we double? 30 minutes. Non-negotiable.

### 11. Measuring Vanity Metrics

**What I did:** Tracked: GitHub stars, Twitter followers, blog views, team size.

**Why:** They go up and feel good.

**Reality:** None correlate with revenue.

**Fix:** Track only: paying customers, revenue, retention, CAC, LTV, NPS. Everything else is noise.

### 12. Not Documenting Decisions

**What I did:** Made architecture choices in Slack calls. No ADRs. No decision log.

**Why:** "We'll remember."

**Reality:** 3 months later: "Why did we choose X?" Nobody knows. Debate restarts.

**Fix:** Every non-trivial decision gets an ADR (Architecture Decision Record):
- Context
- Decision
- Consequences
- Alternatives considered

## The Meta-Mistake

**Thinking mistakes are avoidable.**

They're not. You *will* make them. The goal isn't zero mistakes—it's:
1. **Smaller mistakes** (ship smaller, learn faster)
2. **Faster recognition** (weekly reviews, honest metrics)
3. **Cheaper correction** (reversible decisions, documented rationale)
4. **Learning once** (write it down, share with team)

Every mistake above taught me something I couldn't have learned from a blog post.

But I'd rather you learn from my scars than get your own.
