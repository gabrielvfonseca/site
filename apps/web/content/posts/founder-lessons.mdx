I'm a Computer Engineering student at NOVA in Lisbon. I'm also the founder and CEO of Frontal, an enterprise AI company.

People ask how I do both. The honest answer: I don't "balance" them. I integrate them.

Here's what I've learned building a company while getting a degree.

## Theory Is a Map, Not the Territory

University teaches you distributed systems theory. Frontal teaches you what happens when your agent framework deadlocks in production because a customer's network has 200ms latency spikes.

Both matter. But they're different things.

**What university gave me:**
- How to prove correctness (or find the bug in the proof)
- How databases actually work under the hood
- How to learn fast and structure complex knowledge
- A peer group that pushes me

**What Frontal gave me:**
- How to ship when you don't have all the information
- How to talk to humans who don't care about your architecture
- How to make decisions with 60% data and 40% conviction
- How to protect the team's focus

The engineers I respect most have both. They use theory to avoid rookie mistakes, and practice to know which theory applies.

## Product Is What People Pay For

I used for, Not What You Build

Early Frontal: We built a beautiful agent framework. Clean APIs. Great docs. Type-safe everything.

Customer: "Cool. But can it process our invoice PDFs and reconcile them against our ERP?"

Us: "Well, you'd need to build that on top..."

Customer: "So it doesn't solve my problem?"

That conversation changed everything. We stopped building "AI infrastructure" and started building "invoice reconciliation that happens to use AI."

The technology is the same. The framing is different. The second one gets contracts signed.

## Hire for Slope, Not Intercept

I've hired three engineers so far. None had "AI agent framework" experience. All had:
- Shipped production systems end-to-end
- Owned something from idea to deprecation
- Written code they had to maintain for years
- Asked "why?" before "how?"

I can teach the domain. I can't teach ownership.

The best interview question: "Tell me about a system you built that failed in production. What did you learn?"

The answer reveals more than any LeetCode score.

## Protect the Maker's Schedule

My calendar has two modes:
- **Maker mode (7-11am, 2-6pm):** Deep work. No meetings. No Slack. Phone in another room.
- **Manager mode (11am-2pm, 6pm+):** Calls, emails, decisions, unblocking.

The team knows: if my door is closed (or status is "focus"), it's not personal. It's the only way I write code that doesn't need rewriting.

As founder, I *am* the bottleneck for technical decisions. If I don't protect my thinking time, the whole company gets worse decisions.

## Build in Public, Decide in Private

I write about Frontal's architecture. I open-source our tooling. I share metrics.

But strategy, pricing, hiring, fundraising—those stay private.

Transparency builds trust. Opacity protects leverage. You need both.

The rule: share the *what* and *how*, not the *why* and *when* of business decisions.

## Your First 10 Customers Are Your Product Team

We don't have a PM. Our first 10 customers *are* the PM.

Every feature goes through: "Would Customer X pay more for this? Would Customer Y churn without it?"

If the answer is "maybe" for both, we don't build it.

This only works if you talk to them weekly. Not "user research." Real conversations: "Show me how you do this today. Where does it break? What would you pay to fix?"

## Technical Debt Is a Business Decision

"We should refactor the memory system" is an engineering opinion.

"Refactoring the memory system will unblock the enterprise deal with Company X, which is worth $50k ARR" is a business case.

I make the engineering team speak in business terms. Not because code doesn't matter—because *code that doesn't serve the business is a hobby.*

Sometimes the right answer is "ship the hack, document the debt, fix it next quarter." Sometimes it's "stop everything, this will cost us the customer."

The distinction is judgment. Not purity.

## Fundraising Is a Distraction, Not a Milestone

We raised a pre-seed. It took 6 weeks. During those 6 weeks, product velocity dropped 40%.

The money helped. The *process* hurt.

Next time: raise faster, raise less often, optimize for partner quality over valuation. And never let fundraising become the story you tell yourself about progress.

## The Degree Is the Backup Plan, Not the Distraction

People assume I'll drop out. I won't.

My degree forces me to:
- Learn things I'd never choose (signal processing, control theory)
- Meet people outside the tech bubble
- Have a credential that opens doors if Frontal fails
- Practice time management at a level most founders never learn

The opportunity cost is real. But so is the optionality.

## What I'm Still Figuring Out

- How to scale from "founder does everything" to "team owns outcomes"
- When to hire a real PM vs. keeping product engineering-led
- How to build culture intentionally, not accidentally
- Whether to stay in Lisbon or follow the capital

I'll write about these when I have answers, not opinions.

---

The shortest version: **Build something people pay for. Hire people who own outcomes. Protect your thinking time. Stay a student of both theory and practice.**

Everything else is noise.
